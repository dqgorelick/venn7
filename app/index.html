Venn 7 - Exploring Symmetric 7-Venn Diagrams with a Musical Interface

You may be familiar with the following Venn diagrams, which visualize all possible intersections of two and three sets:

To introduce some clear terminology, the boundary of each set is referred to as a "curve," and the shapes formed by interesctions and differences of curves are the "regions." The 2-Venn diagram has three regions and the 3-Venn diagram seven regions. In general, an n-Venn diagram has exactly 2n - 1 regions. Finally, we'll define the "order" of a region as the number of curves it is contained in.

This is topologically correct, but it's uglier than the 2-Venn and 3-Venn diagrams. We pose two questions:

  1. Can a 4-Venn diagram be created using circles?
  2. Can a 4-Venn diagram possess fourfold rotational symmetry?

Interestingly, the answer to both these question is "no." Although it satisfies neither of the above properties, there is a nice-looking 4-Venn diagram built using four congruent ellipses and possessing reflectional symmetry (Keep Talking players may recognize this one):

As we can gather, Venn diagrams of order 4 or more don't generalize easily from the 2-Venn and 3-Venn diagrams. In fact, we might wonder if Venn diagrams even exist at all for n > 4. The answer to this question was positively resolved by Venn himself, who introduced a construction involving drawing curves around the borders of other curves. It's visually messy, but shows that Venn diagrams exist for all orders:

These are not the only higher-order Venn diagrams, and the space of higher-order Venn diagrams is rich, and has fascinated the field of combinatorics for decades.

One of the most studied topics is symmetric Venn diagrams -- n-Venn diagrams with n-fold rotational symemtry. We've mentioned that no 4-Venn diagram can be symmetric, which follows from Henderson's theorem:

Henderson's theorem: if a symmetric n-Venn diagram exists, then n must be prime.

That brings us to n = 5, which is prime. Grunbaum found the only symmetric 5-Venn diagram in [year].

As more 7-Venn diagrams were found, some special constraints were established to narrow the search:

  1. Simple: No three curves can intersect at one point.
  2. Monotone: The regions sort themselves into concentric layers by order. Formally, each region of order 1 < k < n is bordered by regions of order k + 1 and k - 1. (The above diagram is not monotone, reader exercise to see why.)
  3. Polar symmetric: The Venn diagram is topologically identical if turned inside out.

It was shown that there are exactly six "golden 7-Venn diagrams" that satisfy all three of these properties, named Adelaide, Victoria, Massey, Manawatu, Palmerston North, and Hamilton.

If you are curious about the n = 11 case, Mamakani and Ruskey discovered the first golden 11-Venn diagram, along with over 200,000 more. Unfortunately, these diagrams are less tenable as interactive Web apps, since most of the 2,047 regions are very small.
